Data Ethics and Biases

Objectives

By the end of this lecture you should be able to: - Identify ethical and regulatory considerations in data science projects. - Recognise different types of biases and their impacts. - Evaluate real-world case studies through an ethical and regulatory lens.

Ethics in Data Science

Data science decisions can have profound effects on individuals and society. When data is used carelessly or unethically, it can result in discrimination, breaches of privacy, and a loss of public trust. Practising responsible data science helps prevent harm and supports fairness. Ethical considerations should inform every stage of a project, from data collection to analysis and sharing. Being transparent and accountable is crucial for building trust and ensuring that data science serves the public good.

Case Study: Amazon’s AI Recruiting Tool

Amazon developed an AI recruiting tool to automate the process of reviewing job applicants. However, the system showed bias against women, downgrading resumes that included terms like “women’s” or were from all-women colleges. This bias stemmed from training data that reflected historical male dominance in tech roles. Despite attempts to fix the issue, Amazon ultimately scrapped the tool, highlighting the risks of relying on biased data and the importance of careful oversight in AI systems.

For more information see this news article.

Ethics by Design

Understanding Bias in Data Science

The main cause of the previous case study was a bias in the data set that was unaccounted for. Understanding the kinds of bias that can affect our analyses can help with mitigating the biases. Some examples:

  • Sampling Bias: Data collected is not representative of the population intended to be analyzed.
  • Selection Bias: Certain groups are systematically excluded or included, skewing results.
  • Measurement Bias: Errors in how data is collected or measured, leading to inaccurate results.
  • Confirmation Bias: Tendency to interpret or select data that confirms pre-existing beliefs.
  • Algorithmic Bias: Models produce systematically prejudiced results due to biased training data or design.

In the Amazon example above there was a selection bias: because the training set consisted largely of male candidates, the trained algorithm exhibited the same bias towards male candidates.

Assessing Biases

In general, when we train machine learning algorithms, we are looking for algorithms that identify a bias in a training set (e.g. “candidates with higher scores in their data science course at University tend to do better in their jobs”). There is a difference between this kind of bias, where the algorithm identifies a genuine relationship relevant to job performance, and harmful biases, such as discrimination.

The key distinction is whether the bias reflects a legitimate, job-related factor or perpetuates unfair treatment based on protected characteristics. Responsible data science requires us to critically assess which biases are acceptable (e.g., those that improve prediction based on relevant skills) and which are unethical or illegal (e.g., those that disadvantage groups based on gender, race, or other protected attributes).

Regulatory Considerations

Disclaimer

We cannot discuss the regulatory environment of every country, so we will focus on UK regulations. Moreover this is too complex of a topic to cover thoroughly in a single lecture, and regulations can change. Therefore, when applying in your own work, check the latest regulations and seek expert advice if needed. Always ensure compliance with relevant laws and guidelines, as these may differ depending on your location and the nature of your data.

From the UK Government’s summary of UK GDPR; data must be:

  • used fairly, lawfully and transparently
  • used for specified, explicit purposes
  • used in a way that is adequate, relevant and limited to only what is necessary
  • accurate and, where necessary, kept up to date
  • kept for no longer than is necessary
  • handled in a way that ensures appropriate security, including protection against unlawful or unauthorised processing, access, loss, destruction or damage

Additional legal protection is available for analysis that involves protected characteristics, such as:

  • Race and ethnicity
  • Political opinions and religious beliefs
  • Sex life or orientation

Consequences of Violating GDPR

To ensure that GDPR is adopted by companies, hefty penalties have been attached to non-compliance. Organizations can face fines of up to £17.5 million or 4% of annual global turnover, whichever is higher. Penalties are similar in the EU.

In addition to this there are non-financial repercussions, including:

  • Reputational Damage: Loss of public trust and damage to brand reputation.
  • Legal Action: Individuals affected by data breaches may seek compensation.
  • Operational Disruption: Regulatory investigations can disrupt business operations.

Managing Ethics as a Data Scientist

In the interests of ethics by design, various frameworks have been introduced to support a data scientist, both in terms of documenting potential ethical concerns in datasets and highlighting the relevant features of downstream processors (e.g. AI models). In this class we will focus on data cards and model cards.

Data Cards

The research paper by (Pushkarna, Zaldivar, and Kjartansson 2022) proposed data cards, a framework for documenting datasets that includes thorough documentation of potential ethical considerations. A more digestible summary can be found here. The documentation required by the framework is quite extensive and includes far more information than one would typically have access to even as a data owner; completing a full data card could be challenging, but still represents the current “gold standard” for data documentation. Moreover, not every field needs to be completed in a data card to provide a useful data summary. (For example, Kaggle has its users complete partial data cards, and scores datasets based on the quality of the documentation).

Particularly relevant to ethics is the Sensitivity of Data section (see relevant section here).

Practical Implementation of Data Cards

For this course we will use a minimal version of a data card containing the following fields.

  • Dataset name
  • Version / date
  • Short description (1–2 sentences)
  • Source / provenance (where data comes from; link)
  • Purpose / intended use (what analyses are allowed; forbidden uses)
  • Composition summary (rows, columns, key variables, sample size)
  • Protected attributes present? (yes/no; list)
  • Missingness summary (major variables with missingness)
  • Collection method / measurement details (brief)
  • Known limitations / biases (brief bullets)
  • Privacy/sensitivity (contains personal data? special categories?)
  • Licence / sharing restrictions
  • Contact / owner (name, email)
Example

This is an example model card for the Titanic dataset from Kaggle(https://www.kaggle.com/c/titanic):

  • Dataset name: Titanic (Kaggle / public)
  • Version / date: 2020-11-01
  • Short description: Passenger manifest for Titanic voyage with survival label.
  • Source / provenance: Kaggle Titanic dataset — https://www.kaggle.com/c/titanic
  • Purpose / intended use: Teaching classification; NOT for profiling or real-world commercial decisions.
  • Composition summary: 891 rows, columns include PassengerId, Survived, Pclass, Name, Sex, Age, SibSp, Parch, Ticket, Fare, Cabin, Embarked.
  • Protected attributes present? Yes — sex (gender). Age may be sensitive for some contexts.
  • Missingness summary: Age ~20% missing, Cabin ~77% missing. (For illustration, not accurate)
  • Collection method / measurement details: Historical passenger manifest; some fields manually digitized; possible transcription errors.
  • Known limitations / biases:
    • Not representative of modern populations.
    • Survival correlated with class and gender due to historical boarding policies — not causal for many modern uses.
  • Privacy/sensitivity: Public historical data, low privacy risk, but contains personal names.
  • Licence / sharing restrictions: Unknown
  • Contact / owner: Kaggle
Tip

In assessments you will not be required to complete entire data cards, but you may need to complete small parts of them.

Model Cards

Model cards are standardized documentation frameworks designed to provide transparent information about machine learning models. They summarize key details such as intended use, performance metrics, ethical considerations, limitations, and potential risks. Model cards help stakeholders understand how a model works, its strengths and weaknesses, and any fairness or safety concerns.

While we will not use machine learning models in this course, it is increasingly important for a data scientist to be able to understand the models as a consumer. We will therefore focus on reading and understanding model cards from the fairness and safety perspective.

Example Model Card

Below is a simplified example of a model card for a hypothetical loan approval classifier:

Example Model Card
  • Model name: Loan Approval Classifier v1.0
  • Version / date: 2024-06-01
  • Short description: Predicts loan approval based on applicant financial and demographic data.
  • Intended use: Assisting bank staff in evaluating loan applications; NOT for fully automated decision-making.
  • Performance metrics: Accuracy: 85%, Precision: 80%, Recall: 78% (on validation set)
  • Protected attributes considered: Gender, ethnicity, age (used for fairness analysis, not as predictors)
  • Fairness assessment: Tested for disparate impact; no significant bias detected on gender, but slight bias observed for age group 60+.
  • Limitations: May not generalize to applicants outside the training data region; sensitive to missing income data.
  • Ethical considerations: Should not be used as the sole basis for loan decisions; human review required.
  • Risks: Potential for indirect bias if input features correlate with protected attributes; risk of over-reliance on model outputs.
  • Contact / owner: Data Science Team, Example Bank (contact@examplebank.com)

As a data scientist feeding data into a model, we need to assess whether the model card raises any causes for concern and ensure that our analysis pipeline and stakeholders are aware. In this case we might consider:

  • Attaching warnings to our analysis highlighting the need for human review.
  • Testing for systematic bias, e.g. by classifying records with protected characteristics and assessing empirically whether there is a bias present.
  • Ensuring the model is not extrapolating, by checking how closely input test records correlate with the training set.

Why Model Cards Matter

Model cards promote transparency and accountability by documenting how a model was built, tested, and intended to be used. They help users and stakeholders:

  • Understand the model’s strengths and weaknesses.
  • Identify potential fairness or safety concerns.
  • Make informed decisions about deploying or trusting the model.
  • Ensure compliance with ethical and regulatory standards.

For more details, see Model Cards for Model Reporting and the Google Model Card Toolkit.

Takeaways

Do’s - Do document your data and models using frameworks like data cards and model cards—transparency builds trust and helps identify risks early. - Do actively look for and mitigate biases at every stage, from data collection through to deployment and monitoring.

Don’ts

  • Don’t ignore GDPR and regulatory requirements. Penalties can reach £17.5 million or 4% of global turnover.
  • Don’t assume your data is representative or your model is fair. Always critically assess for limitations and potential harms.
