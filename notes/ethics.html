7  Data Ethics and Biases

Objectives

By the end of this lecture you should be able to: - Identify ethical and regulatory considerations in data science projects. - Recognise different types of biases and their impacts. - Evaluate real-world case studies through an ethical and regulatory lens.

7.1 Ethics in Data Science

7.1.1 Why does Ethics Matter in Data Science?

  • Data science decisions can significantly impact individuals and society.
  • Unethical use of data can lead to discrimination, privacy violations, and loss of trust.
  • Responsible data practices help prevent harm and promote fairness.
  • Ethical considerations guide data collection, analysis, and sharing.
  • Transparency and accountability are essential for trustworthy data science.

Motivate with an applied dataset, give examples of how an analysis could lead to ethical issues.

7.1.2 Understanding Bias in Data Science

Talk about biases that we could encounter in data science and how they could lead to ethical issues if not recognised. Some examples:

  • Sampling Bias: Data collected is not representative of the population intended to be analyzed.
  • Selection Bias: Certain groups are systematically excluded or included, skewing results.
  • Measurement Bias: Errors in how data is collected or measured, leading to inaccurate results.
  • Confirmation Bias: Tendency to interpret or select data that confirms pre-existing beliefs.
  • Algorithmic Bias: Models produce systematically prejudiced results due to biased training data or design.

7.1.3 Regulatory Considerations

Disclaimer

We cannot discuss the regulatory environment of every country, so we will focus on UK regulations. Moreover this is too complex of a topic to cover thoroughly in a single lecture, and regulations can change. Therefore, when applying in your own work, check the latest regulations and seek expert advice if needed. Always ensure compliance with relevant laws and guidelines, as these may differ depending on your location and the nature of your data.

From the UK Government’s summary of UK GDPR; data must be:

  • used fairly, lawfully and transparently
  • used for specified, explicit purposes
  • used in a way that is adequate, relevant and limited to only what is necessary
  • accurate and, where necessary, kept up to date
  • kept for no longer than is necessary
  • handled in a way that ensures appropriate security, including protection against unlawful or unauthorised processing, access, loss, destruction or damage

Additional legal protection is available for analysis that involves protected characteristics, such as:

  • Race and ethnicity
  • Political opinions and religious beliefs
  • Sex life or orientation

7.1.4 Consequences of Violating GDPR

  • Financial Penalties: Organizations can face fines of up to £17.5 million or 4% of annual global turnover, whichever is higher.
  • Reputational Damage: Loss of public trust and damage to brand reputation.
  • Legal Action: Individuals affected by data breaches may seek compensation.
  • Operational Disruption: Regulatory investigations can disrupt business operations.
  • Mandatory Remediation: Organizations may be required to change their data practices or implement corrective measures.
